Refrigerator Not Cooling

Why This Happens

The most common reason a refrigerator stops cooling is a failure in the defrost system. Modern frost-free refrigerators use a defrost heater, defrost thermostat, and defrost timer or control board to periodically melt frost from the evaporator coils. When one of these components fails, ice builds up on the coils and blocks airflow into the refrigerator compartment. The freezer may still feel cold, but the fridge side warms up because cold air cannot circulate properly.

A failed evaporator fan motor is another frequent cause. This fan sits behind the rear panel in the freezer and pushes cold air from the evaporator coils into both the freezer and refrigerator compartments. If the fan motor burns out or the blade becomes obstructed by ice, air stops flowing and the refrigerator temperature rises even while the compressor continues running.

Compressor and sealed-system problems are less common but more serious. A failing compressor may run without building adequate pressure to circulate refrigerant. A refrigerant leak in the evaporator or condenser coils reduces cooling capacity gradually over time. A failed start relay or overload protector can prevent the compressor from starting at all, which means no cooling anywhere in the unit. Dirty condenser coils can also cause the compressor to overheat and shut down prematurely, resulting in intermittent cooling loss.

Signs You Need Professional Repair

  • Refrigerator interior is warm but the freezer still works
  • Compressor runs constantly without the fridge reaching the set temperature
  • Heavy frost or ice buildup visible on the back wall of the freezer
  • Clicking sounds from the compressor area followed by silence
  • Condenser coils are clean but the unit still cannot maintain temperature

How We Fix It

Our technician begins by checking the temperature in both compartments and listening to the compressor and fan motors. We pull the rear panel in the freezer to inspect the evaporator coils for frost buildup, test the defrost heater and thermostat for continuity, and verify the defrost timer or control board is cycling correctly. If the defrost system checks out, we move to the evaporator fan motor, condenser fan motor, and condenser coils.

For compressor-related issues, we test the start relay, overload protector, and compressor windings. If refrigerant levels are low, we check for leaks before recharging. Common parts replaced include the defrost heater, defrost thermostat, defrost control board, evaporator fan motor, condenser fan motor, start relay, and temperature control thermostat. Most repairs are completed within one to two hours.
