Why This Happens

When a freezer stops freezing but the compressor is still running, the problem frequently lies in the evaporator system. The evaporator coils are where refrigerant absorbs heat from the air inside the freezer. If these coils are coated in a thick layer of frost due to a defrost system failure, the refrigerant cannot absorb heat efficiently, and the freezer temperature rises. The defrost heater, defrost thermostat, and defrost timer or control board must all function correctly to keep the coils clear.

A failed evaporator fan motor directly impacts freezer performance. This fan circulates air across the evaporator coils and distributes it throughout the freezer and refrigerator compartments. When the motor fails, cold air cannot reach the food even though the coils are cold. You may notice that areas right next to the evaporator coils are still cold, but the rest of the freezer is warm. Some models have multiple fans, and a failure in any one of them can create uneven cooling.

Compressor and sealed-system issues cause the most serious freezing failures. If the compressor is running but not building adequate pressure, refrigerant does not circulate properly and the evaporator coils cannot get cold enough. A refrigerant leak gradually reduces the system's cooling capacity, causing a slow decline in freezer temperature over days or weeks. A failed compressor start relay can also prevent the compressor from running at all, which means neither the freezer nor the refrigerator will cool. Additionally, a damaged door gasket allows warm air to enter continuously, forcing the system to work beyond its capacity.

Signs You Need Professional Repair

  • Ice cream is soft or food is partially thawed
  • Freezer thermometer reads above 10 degrees Fahrenheit
  • Heavy frost or ice buildup on the back wall of the freezer
  • Compressor runs continuously without cycling off
  • Freezer door gasket is visibly worn, torn, or not sealing tightly

How We Fix It

Our technician measures the freezer temperature and inspects the evaporator coils for frost accumulation. If the coils are iced over, we test the defrost heater for continuity, check the defrost thermostat, and verify the defrost timer or control board is initiating defrost cycles on schedule. We test the evaporator fan motor for proper operation and check the condenser coils for dust and debris that could reduce system efficiency.

For compressor-related failures, we test the start relay, run capacitor, and compressor windings. If a refrigerant leak is suspected, we perform a sealed-system evaluation and discuss repair options. Common parts replaced include the defrost heater, defrost thermostat, defrost control board, evaporator fan motor, start relay, condenser fan motor, door gasket, and temperature control thermostat. Most freezer repairs are completed in one to two hours.
