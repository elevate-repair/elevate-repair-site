Dryer Overheating in Denver?

An overheating dryer is not just an inconvenience -- it is a safety hazard. When your dryer gets excessively hot, scorches clothing, or shuts off mid-cycle from high temperatures, you need professional repair immediately. Why This Happens

The leading cause of dryer overheating is a clogged or restricted exhaust vent. Your dryer is designed to push hot, moist air out through the exhaust duct to the outside of your home. When lint accumulates inside the vent line, or when the duct is kinked, crushed, or too long, that hot air has nowhere to go. It backs up into the dryer cabinet, causing internal temperatures to climb well above the normal operating range of 125 to 135 degrees Fahrenheit. This creates a dangerous situation because lint is highly flammable, and the combination of excessive heat and trapped lint is the leading cause of dryer fires in the United States.

A failed cycling thermostat is another common cause of overheating. The cycling thermostat is the component that monitors the air temperature inside the drum and cycles the heating element on and off to maintain a consistent temperature. When this thermostat fails in the closed position, it no longer shuts off the heater when the target temperature is reached, and the heating element runs continuously. The defective high-limit thermostat is a related but separate issue. This safety thermostat is designed to cut power to the heating element if the dryer exceeds a maximum temperature threshold. If the high-limit thermostat fails, there is no backup to prevent the dryer from reaching dangerously high temperatures.

In electric dryers, a heating element that has shorted to the case can also cause overheating. Over time, the heating element coil can sag from repeated heating and cooling cycles. If the coil touches the metal housing that surrounds it, it creates a ground fault that allows the element to stay energized even when the thermostat sends a signal to turn it off. Damaged felt drum seals are a less obvious cause: these seals prevent hot air from escaping around the drum edges. When they wear through or tear, heated air bypasses the normal airflow path and concentrates in spots within the cabinet, causing localized overheating and potential scorching of clothes that contact the drum edges.

Signs You Need Professional Repair

  • The outside of the dryer cabinet is extremely hot to the touch during operation
  • Clothes come out with scorch marks, brown spots, or a burning smell
  • The dryer shuts off mid-cycle and will not restart until it cools down
  • The laundry room becomes noticeably hotter than usual when the dryer runs
  • The thermal fuse has blown more than once in the past year

How We Fix It

Because overheating is a safety concern, we treat these calls with urgency. Our technician begins by measuring the exhaust temperature at the vent termination point and comparing it to the manufacturer's specifications. We then disconnect the exhaust duct and inspect it for lint accumulation, crushing, or improper routing. If the vent is restricted, we clear the blockage and verify airflow has been restored to safe levels. We also check the vent run length and number of elbows to ensure it meets building code requirements, since an overly long or convoluted vent path creates ongoing overheating risk.

Next, we test the cycling thermostat, high-limit thermostat, and heating element. The cycling thermostat is tested for proper opening and closing temperatures using a multimeter and heat source. The high-limit thermostat is checked for continuity and correct trip temperature. For the heating element, we inspect it visually for sagging coils that may be contacting the housing, and we test for a ground fault between the element terminals and the metal case. If the felt drum seals are worn, we replace them to restore proper airflow channeling around the drum perimeter.
