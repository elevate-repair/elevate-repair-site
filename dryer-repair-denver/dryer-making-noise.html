Why Is My Dryer Making Loud Noises?

Squealing, thumping, grinding, or rattling from a dryer usually points to worn mechanical components inside the cabinet. This guide explains the most common noise sources, how to narrow down which part is failing, and what a technician examines during diagnosis.

Root Causes of Dryer Noise

Worn Drum Rollers: The drum rides on two or more rubber-coated rollers mounted on the rear bulkhead (and sometimes the front). With age, the rubber develops flat spots or hardens from heat, causing a rhythmic thumping or rumbling that tracks with drum speed. Worn roller shafts can also wobble, producing scraping against the drum.

Glazed or Fraying Drum Belt: The belt wraps around the drum and loops through the idler pulley and motor to drive rotation. An aging belt develops surface glazing or cracks that cause it to slip, producing a high-pitched squeal — especially under a heavy load. A fraying belt makes a flapping or slapping sound before it eventually breaks.

Failing Idler Pulley: The idler pulley maintains tension on the drum belt. Its internal bearing wears over time, creating a steady squeaking or squealing throughout the entire cycle regardless of how much laundry is inside. This is one of the most common dryer noise causes in machines over five years old.

Worn Drum Glides: Plastic or felt pads on the front bulkhead support the drum's front edge as it rotates. When these wear through, metal contacts metal and produces a loud scraping or grinding noise. This typically worsens over weeks rather than appearing suddenly.

Blower Wheel Obstruction or Damage: Small items that pass through the lint trap — coins, buttons, hairpins, zipper pulls — can reach the blower wheel and cause rattling, clicking, or fluttering sounds. Cracked blower fins produce a persistent whirring or vibration that doesn't stop as the dryer runs.

Visual Inspection Steps

Listen to timing: Does the noise follow a rhythm tied to the drum's rotation speed, or is it constant regardless of how fast the drum spins? Rhythmic thumping tied to drum speed points to drum rollers or drum imbalance. A constant squeal at a fixed pitch throughout the cycle points to the idler pulley. Scraping that's always present suggests drum glides.

Listen to location: Stand beside the dryer and try to identify whether the noise comes from the front, rear, or bottom of the cabinet. Rear noises typically indicate drum rollers or the drive motor. Front noises suggest drum glides or the front bearing. Sounds from below the drum area point toward the motor, idler pulley, or blower.

Check the lint trap area: Shine a flashlight into the lint trap opening. Look for debris below the screen — coins, clips, and buttons are common. If you recently dried clothing with items in the pockets and the rattling started afterward, a blower obstruction is the likely cause.

Safe DIY Checks

Empty pockets before every load. Coins and metal objects are the most common cause of sudden rattling and blower damage. This is the simplest prevention step.

Run the dryer empty for one cycle. If the noise disappears or significantly reduces without laundry inside, the cause may be load-related (unbalanced items, zippers hitting the drum). If the noise persists with an empty drum, the issue is mechanical — rollers, belt, idler pulley, or blower.

Inspect the accessible exterior. Check that the dryer sits level on the floor and isn't vibrating against a wall or cabinet. A slightly unleveled dryer can develop a rhythmic thump that mimics roller noise. Adjust the leveling feet if needed.

Safety Notes

Do not continue running a dryer that produces grinding or loud scraping noises. Worn drum glides and failing rollers can cause the drum to drop or shift, damaging the drum itself and making what was a minor repair much more expensive. Never attempt to oil drum rollers — lubricants attract lint and create a fire hazard inside a heat-producing appliance. Always unplug the dryer before opening the cabinet for any inspection.

When to Call a Professional

Professional diagnosis is appropriate when: the noise has persisted across multiple loads and isn't coming from debris; the squeal or thump is progressively getting louder; the dryer starts normally but the noise is accompanied by reduced tumbling (belt or roller near failure); or you cannot identify the source location after basic listening. Continuing to run a dryer with failing drum rollers or a fraying belt risks a complete breakdown mid-cycle.

Professional Repair Process

A technician begins by running the dryer briefly to listen and identify the sound pattern — timing, location, and character. They then open the cabinet to inspect the drum rollers for flat spots or shaft play, check the belt for glazing or fraying, test the idler pulley by hand for smooth rotation, inspect drum glides for wear-through, and examine the blower wheel for debris or cracked fins.

Drum rollers are replaced as a complete set — replacing only the noisiest one leaves others at different wear levels, leading to a quick return of the problem. The drum belt and idler pulley are typically replaced together since they wear at comparable rates. After repair, a full test cycle with a load confirms quiet operation before the job is considered complete. Frequently Asked Questions

Does the type of noise tell me which part is failing? Yes, generally. A rhythmic thump matching drum speed = drum rollers. A steady squeal throughout the cycle = idler pulley bearing. A scraping or grinding = drum glides or roller shaft. A rattling from below the drum = blower wheel debris or cracked fin. These aren't guarantees, but they point the diagnosis in the right direction before any disassembly.

Is it safe to keep using a noisy dryer? For minor squeaking, short-term use while arranging repair is usually acceptable. For grinding or heavy scraping, stop using the dryer. Worn-through glides create metal-to-metal contact that can score the drum edge and turn a $50 parts repair into a drum replacement. A fraying belt can snap mid-cycle, leaving laundry damp and requiring a service visit regardless.

How long do drum rollers typically last? In most dryers, drum rollers last 8–12 years under normal household use. High-usage households (multiple loads daily) may see roller wear in 5–7 years. The rubber compound used in rollers hardens from repeated heat exposure, so the age of the machine matters more than total cycle count in many cases.
