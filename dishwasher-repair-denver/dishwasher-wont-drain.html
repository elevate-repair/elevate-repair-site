Dishwasher Won't Drain: Causes, Diagnosis & Professional Solutions

Standing water in your dishwasher tub after a cycle means something is blocking the drain path. The problem could be a failed pump, a clogged hose, or a stuck valve. We'll help you understand what's happening and decide whether you can fix it yourself or need professional help.

Main Causes of Drain Failure

The dishwasher's drain system includes three main components: the drain pump (which forces water out), the drain hose (which carries water away), and the check valve (which prevents backflow). When water won't leave the tub, the blockage is in one of these areas.

Failed drain pump: The drain pump's small motor pushes used water out through the hose. When the motor windings burn out, the impeller cracks, or the internal seal fails, the pump can no longer move water. You may hear a humming sound as the motor attempts to engage but fails, or complete silence if the motor has seized. A failed pump is a common cause of no-drain issues and usually requires replacement.

Clogged drain hose: Food particles, grease, and mineral deposits accumulate inside the corrugated drain hose over time. Denver's hard water especially contributes to mineral buildup that narrows the hose interior. Blockages also happen at connection points—where the hose meets the garbage disposal or sink drain. If a garbage disposal was recently installed, the knockout plug inside the disposal inlet may not have been removed, completely blocking the dishwasher drain line. This is actually quite common after appliance upgrades and is easily fixed.

Stuck check valve: The check valve is a one-way flapper that lets water exit but prevents it from flowing backward into the tub. When mineral deposits or debris get stuck in this valve, it can't open, and water becomes trapped. This is especially common in areas with hard water like Denver.

Drain solenoid failure: Some dishwashers use an electrically operated solenoid valve to control when drainage begins. If the solenoid fails electrically or mechanically, the valve stays closed and water never gets the signal to drain. This typically occurs on older models and shows up as error codes on the display.

If your dishwasher is also leaking water, the drainage issue may be secondary to a seal problem. Conversely, if dishes aren't cleaning properly, drainage problems can prevent fresh water from entering. These issues sometimes overlap.

Diagnosing the Drainage Problem: Step-by-Step

Listen to the drain cycle: Run your dishwasher on a short cycle and pay attention during the drain phase. Do you hear the drain pump motor running (a whirring or buzzing sound)? If you hear nothing, the pump may have failed. If you hear humming but no water movement, the pump motor is trying but can't generate enough force—likely a mechanical failure or severe blockage.

Check for water backup: If water backs up into your sink when the dishwasher drains, the blockage is in the shared drain line, not the dishwasher itself. This is often a sign of a clogged sink drain or garbage disposal line, not a dishwasher-specific problem.

Smell the tub: Stagnant water in the dishwasher tub creates an unpleasant odor within hours. The presence of odor confirms that water is sitting in the machine and not draining. This also accelerates mold and bacterial growth, so prompt repair is important.

Look at the error code: Modern dishwashers display error codes when they detect drain problems. Write down any code you see—it often points to a specific component failure (drain solenoid, pump, or timer issue). Share this code with your repair technician.

What You Can Safely Check (DIY Diagnostics)

Check the drain hose for kinks: Turn off and unplug the dishwasher. Look under the unit (from the side if accessible) and visually inspect the drain hose path. Is it kinked, pinched, or routed uphill? The drain hose should slope downward toward the drain connection. If you find a kink, straightening it may restore drainage—but do this only when the machine is completely off and cool.

Inspect the garbage disposal: If your dishwasher drains to a garbage disposal, check that the knockout plug was removed when the disposal was installed. You can see inside the disposal inlet by removing the rubber splash guard. If a solid plug is still there, that's your problem.

Clean the filter trap: Most dishwashers have a small filter at the bottom that traps food particles. Some are removable and cleanable. Check your manual—if the filter is accessible and visibly clogged, cleaning it might help, though it typically isn't the cause of complete drain failure.

Do NOT attempt: Taking apart the pump housing, replacing internal drain valves, or disassembling the drain motor. These require specialized tools and knowledge. Opening sealed pump compartments can allow water into electrical components.

When Professional Repair Is Needed

If you hear the pump trying but water doesn't move, a mechanical pump failure or severe blockage requires professional tools to diagnose accurately. If water backs up into the sink, the issue is in the shared drain line—a plumber may be needed alongside dishwasher repair. If error codes appear, the problem likely involves the control board or solenoid, which require specialized diagnostic equipment. If the tub has been standing full of water for more than a few hours, mold and bacterial growth are setting in—prompt professional repair prevents health risks and odor.

Professional Repair Process

Our technician will run a drain cycle and listen closely to the pump motor, checking whether it's engaging and how much force it's generating. We'll inspect the entire drain path from pump outlet through hose to the sink connection, looking for kinks, blockages, and improper routing. We'll test the check valve, verify hose connections, and if the unit has a drain solenoid, test it for electrical function. If needed, we'll clear mineral deposits from the hose and valve using specialized equipment.

Common repairs include clearing mineral and food blockages, replacing a failed drain pump motor, installing a new check valve, correcting improper hose routing, and removing forgotten knockout plugs from new garbage disposals. Safety & Urgency Notes

Standing water in a dishwasher tub becomes a breeding ground for mold and bacteria within 24 hours. If the tub has been full for more than a day, we recommend professional repair within 48 hours to prevent mold damage and odor problems. Do not run additional cycles or try to force water out by tilting the machine.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use my dishwasher if it's not draining? No. Running additional cycles when the tub already has standing water will overflow and potentially damage your kitchen floor and cabinetry. Stop using it until it's repaired.

How much does drain repair usually cost? Most drain repairs run $150–$400, depending on whether it's a simple hose cleaning ($150–$200) or a pump replacement ($300–$400). Our $89 diagnostic fee applies toward the repair cost.

Is drain pump failure covered by warranty? Most manufacturer warranties cover pump failure for 1–3 years. Our 60-day warranty covers all parts and labor on repairs we perform.

Why does my new garbage disposal prevent my dishwasher from draining? The knockout plug inside the disposal inlet must be removed to allow the dishwasher line to connect. If your dishwasher stopped draining after a new disposal installation, that's almost certainly the cause—an easy fix.

Can mineral buildup cause drain failure? Yes, especially in Denver where water is hard. Mineral deposits accumulate in the check valve and drain hose over years of use. We can clear these deposits, but if the buildup is severe, valve replacement may be needed.
