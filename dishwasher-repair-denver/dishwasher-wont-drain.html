Why This Happens

When your dishwasher finishes a cycle but leaves a pool of dirty water sitting in the tub, the problem almost always lies somewhere along the drain path. The most common cause is a failed drain pump. The drain pump uses a small motor to push used water out of the tub and through the drain hose. When the motor windings burn out or the impeller cracks, the pump can no longer expel water, and it simply sits at the bottom of the machine. You may hear a humming sound as the motor tries and fails to engage, or you may hear nothing at all if the motor has completely seized.

A clogged drain hose is another frequent cause. Food particles, grease, and soap scum accumulate inside the corrugated hose over time, gradually narrowing the passage until water can't pass through. The blockage can also occur where the drain hose connects to the garbage disposal or the sink drain tailpiece. If a new garbage disposal was recently installed, the knockout plug inside the disposal inlet may not have been removed, completely blocking the dishwasher drain line.

Less obvious causes include a stuck check valve and a faulty drain solenoid. The check valve is a one-way flapper that prevents drained water from flowing back into the tub. If it gets stuck in the closed position due to debris or mineral buildup, water has nowhere to go. On some older models, a drain solenoid opens a gate valve at the start of the drain cycle. If that solenoid fails electrically or mechanically, the valve stays shut and water stays trapped in the machine.

Signs You Need Professional Repair

  • Standing water remains in the bottom of the dishwasher after the cycle completes
  • The dishwasher makes a humming sound during drain but water doesn't move
  • Water backs up into the sink when the dishwasher drains
  • An unpleasant odor comes from the dishwasher due to stagnant water
  • The dishwasher stops mid-cycle and displays a drain error code

How We Fix It

Our technician begins by running a drain cycle and listening to the drain pump to determine whether the motor is engaging. We then inspect the entire drain path, from the pump outlet through the drain hose to the connection at the garbage disposal or sink drain. We check for clogs, kinks, and improper hose routing, and we verify that the check valve moves freely. If the unit has a drain solenoid, we test it for electrical continuity and mechanical function.

Repairs typically involve clearing the blockage, replacing the drain pump motor, installing a new check valve, or correcting the hose routing. If a new garbage disposal was installed without removing the knockout plug, we take care of that as well.
