Why This Happens

Dishwashers use heat during the final rinse and drying phases to evaporate moisture from your dishes. The most common cause of poor drying is a failed heating element. The heating element is a metal loop at the bottom of the tub that heats the air and water during the drying cycle. Over time, the element can burn out, develop a break in its coil, or lose its electrical connection. When this happens, the drying phase runs without any heat, and dishes come out dripping wet. You can often spot a failed element by looking for visible cracks, blistering, or dark burn marks on the coil.

The vent assembly is another critical component in the drying process. During the drying phase, a vent opens in the dishwasher door or inner panel to allow hot, moist air to escape the tub. If the vent flap gets stuck in the closed position due to a broken spring, warped plastic, or debris, that steam stays trapped inside the tub and condenses right back onto your dishes. Some dishwashers also have a small fan motor behind the vent that actively pushes moist air out of the tub. If this fan motor fails, airflow stalls and drying performance drops significantly.

The rinse aid dispenser plays a larger role in drying than many people realize. Rinse aid reduces the surface tension of water, causing it to sheet off dishes rather than forming droplets that cling and leave spots. If the dispenser mechanism is broken, clogged, or empty, water beads on dish surfaces and takes much longer to evaporate. Finally, the high-limit thermostat monitors the temperature inside the tub and can shut off the heating element prematurely if it reads an incorrect temperature. A faulty thermostat may cut the heat before dishes are fully dry, leaving them damp at the end of the cycle.

Signs You Need Professional Repair

  • Dishes and glasses are dripping wet or covered in water droplets after the cycle finishes
  • The inside of the dishwasher tub feels cool to the touch right after a heated dry cycle
  • Excessive steam or moisture appears on the kitchen countertop near the dishwasher
  • The rinse aid indicator shows full but dishes still don't dry properly
  • Plastic items on the top rack are consistently wetter than ceramic or glass items

How We Fix It

Our technician tests the drying system from start to finish. We use a multimeter to check the heating element for continuity, verifying whether current flows through the coil. We inspect the vent assembly to confirm it opens and closes properly, and if the unit has a vent fan motor, we test it for operation. We also examine the rinse aid dispenser mechanism for clogs or mechanical failure and test the high-limit thermostat to make sure it reads the correct temperature and doesn't prematurely cut off the heating element.

Repairs commonly include replacing the heating element, installing a new vent assembly or vent fan motor, clearing or replacing the rinse aid dispenser, or swapping out a faulty high-limit thermostat.
