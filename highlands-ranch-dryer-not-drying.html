Dryer Repair Considerations for Highlands Ranch Homes

Long duct runs through Highlands Ranch townhomes and paired homes are the primary cause of not-drying complaints in the community. In many Firelight, Northridge, and Eastridge units, the laundry closet is interior—away from any exterior wall—requiring exhaust duct runs of 20 to 30 feet with multiple elbows. Each 90-degree elbow adds the equivalent of 5 feet of duct resistance, and a flexible duct that has sagged or kinked at any point can double the effective restriction. We measure airflow at the termination cap and inspect for sags in the flexible section before testing the dryer's heating system.

Highlands Ranch's elevation and dry climate combine to accelerate lint accumulation in dryer ducts. Low-humidity air causes fabrics to shed more lint per cycle, and fine particulate matter from Front Range high-elevation soil mixes with lint to form sticky plugs at duct bends. A Highlands Ranch dryer that takes 90 minutes to dry a normal load but shows a normal lint trap is almost always carrying a mid-duct restriction, not a mechanical fault. We clean from the exterior termination end using a rotating brush system on these calls.

Moisture sensor fouling affects not-drying performance in Highlands Ranch's builder-grade dryer stock. The two metal sensor bars inside the drum rely on conductivity across wet fabric to detect remaining moisture. Fabric softener sheet residue and mineral deposits from Highlands Ranch's hard water supply coat these bars over cycles, reducing sensitivity. A dryer that stops an auto-dry cycle with damp clothes still inside—or runs endlessly without signaling done—usually has fouled sensor bars. We clean the sensor bars as part of every Highlands Ranch not-drying call where duct airflow tests normal.
